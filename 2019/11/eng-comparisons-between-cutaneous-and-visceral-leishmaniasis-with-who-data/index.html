[ENG] Comparisons between cutaneous and visceral Leishmaniasis with WHO data

November 22, 2019

I recently found out that the World Health Organization (WHO) has a package available for R in CRAN. CRAN is an archive of packages, codes and documents in general for this programming language. Although the WHO package is not official, it contains very interesting data from this agency.

Installation of necessary packages:

library(WHO)
library(dplyr)
library(tidyverse)
library(ggplot2)
library(lubridate)

codes <- get_codes(extra = TRUE)
glimpse(codes)

3287 datasets on various topics are automatically downloaded with this package, some of them on leishmaniasis. I found this interesting, since this year I had to do research on this disease and on the National Leishmaniasis Program for my “Public Health II” class in the Faculty of Veterinary Sciences of the University of Buenos Aires.

This disease is zoonotic, anthropozoonotic, vectorial and distributed worldwide. It is caused by a parasitic protozoan of the genus Leishmania, which comprises over 20 different species. It is classified as a neglected tropical disease (“NTD”). It presents in three main forms: visceral leishmaniasis, cutaneous leishmaniasis and mucocutaneous leishmaniasis. In this case, I will only analyze the visceral (most severe) and cutaneous (most frequent) forms.

The cycle occurs as follows:

Source: CDC (Center for Disease Control and Prevention).

Dogs are the main reservoir of this disease, at least in Latin America and especially for the visceral form. When they are bitten by female phlebotomus, due to their close relationship with humans, they perpetuate the Leishmania cycle. For example, in this 2010 guide, I find that according to data from the Ministry of Health of Misiones, in Posadas alone there are about 100 thousand dogs, half of which are infected. Most worryingly, only 20% are receiving veterinary treatment or being monitored, which increases the risk of being bitten by a vector and transmitting the disease to humans. Faced with the advance of this disease, the health authorities launched a measure that sparked discontent: following WHO guidelines, they recommended “sacrificing dogs that have been proven to be parasitologically positive, as they can act as a source of infection of vectors and thus of leishmaniasis in humans”. This aroused the indignation of several animal associations, and 200 people marched through the streets of Posadas to condemn the killing of a thousand dogs and demand “a prudent, serious response, without sacrificing animals”. In other words, it seems that the people of Misiones do not accept euthanasia as a solution.

To begin, I download and clean the datasets:

leish_cut <- get_data("NTD_LEISHCNUM") %>%
        select(- c(publishstate, gho)) %>% 
        filter(value != "No data") %>% 
        mutate(value = as.numeric(value))

leish_visc <- get_data("NTD_LEISHVNUM") %>%
        select(- c(publishstate, gho)) %>% 
        filter(value != "No data") %>% 
        mutate(value = as.numeric(value))

Sources:

https://www.who.int/data/gho/indicator-metadata-registry/imr-details/3760

With these datasets, I can observe the distribution from the macro (global) to a slightly more micro level (in this case, Argentina).

First, I want to get a global overview of cutaneous leishmaniasis, by region:

leish_cut %>%
        group_by(region, year) %>%
        summarise(value = sum(value)) %>%
        ggplot(aes(x = year, y = value, color = region, linetype = region)) +
        geom_line(size = 1) +
        theme_light(9) +
        scale_x_continuous(breaks = NULL) +
        labs(x = "Year", y = "Sum of cases\n",
             linetype = NULL, color = NULL,
             title = "Cases of cutaneous leishmaniasis (by region)",
             subtitle = "Years 2005-2017\n",
             caption = "Source: https://www.who.int/") +
        scale_linetype_discrete(guide = FALSE) +
        scale_color_discrete(name = NULL)

This chart shows that the region with the most cases is the Middle East, with a large increase in this time range and a decrease towards 2017. On the WHO website, I find that 70% of the world’s cutaneous leishmaniasis cases are concentrated in this region. Apparently, none of these regions is free of this disease.

Before moving on to a regional view, I want to see the distribution throughout the Americas.

In the case of cutaneous leishmaniasis, the represented countries are Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador, El Salvador, Guatemala, Guyana, Honduras, Mexico, Nicaragua, Panama, Paraguay, Peru, Suriname, United States and Venezuela.

In the case of visceral leishmaniasis, the represented countries are Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Mexico, Nicaragua, Paraguay and Venezuela.

In order to know if there is any problem regarding the representation of countries or if only endemic countries are represented, I looked for the epidemiological situation of each country with respect to the two forms of the disease. Apparently, the dataset only contains those countries that are endemic… It makes sense.

Cutaneous leishmaniasis is more prevalent than visceral leishmaniasis in the Americas, with more than 70,000 cases annually. Visceral leishmaniasis does not reach 5,000 cases per year, but it is much more serious. According to this WHO webpage, “in more than 95% of the cases it is fatal if not treated. It is characterized by irregular episodes of fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly and anemia”. The cutaneous form, on the other hand, “produces cutaneous lesions, especially ulcers, in the exposed areas of the body, which leave life-long scars and are a cause of serious disability”.

For this form of the disease, prevalence is much lower, and has experienced a downturn in recent years. It is important to remember that, unlike cutaneous leishmaniasis, this form of the disease is highly fatal. I wonder if Argentine people would accept the sacrifice of Leishmania-positive animals as a containment measure if they were aware of how deadly this form is.

Again, the National Government’s webpage for this disease discusses symptoms, treatment and prevention, but doesn’t say much about epidemiology. At least, in this case it mentions in which provinces cases usually occur (Misiones, Formosa, Corrientes and Santiago del Estero). Visceral leishmaniasis has a very complete guideline for health teams, published in 2010 by the Ministry of Health.

I would love to find a more detailed dataset on the spatial distribution of cases by province but unfortunately there isn’t one.

Conclusions

Based on this analysis, I believe that it would be useful to carry out some veterinary actions with regard to this disease. Although there is a National Leishmaniasis Program, created in 1999, it could be considered outdated. Therefore, I propose establishing a new National Leishmaniasis Program, in order to improve the early diagnosis of leishmaniasis in canines living in endemic areas of Argentina, in order to prevent the need for euthanasia.

On the other hand, awareness of the disease by dog owners can change the prevalence of the disease in the medium or long term, since they are the largest reservoir. For this reason, a public policy could be writing a poster or leaflet aimed at dog owners that provides education about the signs of the disease, to be placed in the waiting rooms of veterinary clinics. The expected feedback would be an increment in the number of consultations with trained professionals for symptomatology compatible with leishmaniasis, in order to raise the number of diagnosed cases.

It is also important to achieve correct control of vectors and reservoirs. For reservoirs, it is necessary to dispose of garbage frequently, have early detection of canines with leishmaniasis, conduct a rigorous serological control of pets going from endemic areas to disease-free areas, and control rodents through physical (traps, glues, etc.) or chemical methods (fumigants, repellents, etc.). There are also other hygiene issues, such as the disposal of everything used to draw samples (syringes, needles, slides, etc.) and the fluids to be analyzed. Likewise, if a body has to be disposed of, a company specializing in cremation must be hired. In order to control vectors, it is necessary to ensure proper hygiene in and around the home, place mosquito nets in the homes, use long-lasting repellents, control public ponds, and avoid exposure at dusk and midnight, which is the time of greatest activity for the phlebotomus, as stated by the National Administration of Laboratories and Health Institutes (ANLIS) on its website.

If all these actions are implemented, there could be a gradual reduction of this pathology in our country.
