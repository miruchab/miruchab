[ENG] Observation of outbreaks according to FAOSTAT

November 28, 2019

Recently, at 2 a.m. on a weekday, I found myself looking for datasets with veterinary interest… I’ll let you readers decide whether this makes me cool or quite the opposite!

I came across the website for EMPRES, the Emergency Preventive System of the FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization). In this website, you can find information on many livestock and food-borne diseases.

After a very simple search (I only filtered for the country of observation being Argentina), I came across a dataset that I found interesting, since it details all the reports for these diseases recorded by the FAO.

A note: it is likely that not all cases have been reported as required by statute, and therefore, this dataset may not be 100% representative of all the cases present in Argentina. It would be interesting to know how many cases of each disease actually existed and why they were not reported.

Map of the Province of Buenos Aires

The first interesting thing to do is to observe the cases reported in the Province of Buenos Aires (which is where most of the reports made in the entire Argentine territory are concentrated).

In this case, the criterion used is the source of the report, whether it comes from national authorities or from the OIE (international).

ba_map <- get_googlemap(center = c(-60, -36), zoom = 7)

ggmap(ba_map) +
        geom_point(aes(x = longitude, y = latitude,  colour = source), 
                   data = fao, size = 7, alpha = 0.8) +
        labs(title = "Distribution of reports in Buenos Aires",
             subtitle = "By report source\n",
             x = "Longitude",
             y = "Latitude") +
        scale_color_manual(values = c("mediumpurple4", "mediumturquoise"),
                name = "Source")

We can see that between 2006 and 2018 there are 13 reports of diseases with veterinary interest. However, we don’t know which are the diseases involved. To find that out, we can create another chart.

Another map of the Province of Buenos Aires

ba_map2 <- get_googlemap(center = c(-58.5, -34.5), zoom = 8)

ggmap(ba_map2) +
        geom_point(aes(x = longitude, y = latitude,  colour = disease), 
                   data = fao, size = 7, alpha = 0.8) +
        labs(title = "Distribtion of reports in Buenos Aires",
             subtitle = "By disease\n",
             x = "Longitude",
             y = "Latitude") +
         scale_color_discrete(name = "Disease")

We can see that:

  1. There was a report of anthrax (so scary!). According to the OIE, this disease is present in our country. I must admit that I didn’t recall this information, and that it worries me a lot.

  2. At least in Buenos Aires, there were no reports of foot and mouth disease (a disease Argentina is considered free of, with vaccination north of the 42nd parallel and no vaccination south of it).

  3. Some reports of equine diseases such as equine influenza (also known as equine flu, which is classified as “suspicious” according to the OIE) and West Nile fever (of which Argentina has been free since 2006).

  4. Two reports of swine influenza, which was particularly challenging in 2009 due to a pandemic and due to it being a zoonotic disease.

In my opinion, the following most interesting point would be to know when these reports were made, to make sure that there were no cases once the territory was declared free of a certain disease. To do so, we’ll try to make a chart as a function of time.

ggplot(fao) +
        geom_point(aes(x = observationDate, y = disease, color = disease), size = 8, alpha = 0.2) + 
        labs(title = "Reported diseases",
            subtitle = "In Argentina\n",
            y = NULL,
            x = NULL,
            caption = "Source: http://empres-i.fao.org/eipws3g/",
            fill = NULL) +
        theme_minimal() +
        scale_x_date(breaks = fao$observationDate,
                     date_breaks = "1 year",
                     date_labels = "%Y") +
        theme(legend.position = "none",
              axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 90, size = 8))

It can be observed that:

  1. The last reported cases of West Nile fever occurred in 2006, which makes sense, since Argentina has been considered free of this disease since then.

  2. The 2009 swine flu pandemic is also shown very clearly. Those who live in the Argentine territory will surely remember it, since it was widely covered in the media.

  3. Regarding equine influenza, in April 2018 there was an outbreak of the H3N8 serotype. Although the disease is endemic in Argentina, it does not usually appear in the form of outbreaks. This is why this outbreak was considered significant enough to be reported to the OIE. By the following month, “the epidemic had already subsided” according to SENASA reports.

  4. Even the last foot and mouth disease outbreak reported in Argentina in 2006 can be seen. Since then, Argentina has been considered free, with vaccination north of the 42nd parallel and no vaccination south of it.

  5. As for anthrax, information is hard to come by. During 2013 and 2014, 39 outbreaks were identified in cattle in the Province of Buenos Aires. In 2013 alone, 7 human cases of cutaneous anthrax were reported in this province. In 2014, a human death caused by anthrax was reported.

Analyzing this dataset made me question how much the cases are reported and why. There are diseases of those mentioned that are known by the Argentine population, as is the case of the Swine Influenza. But in other cases, such as Anthrax or Foot and Mouth Disease, I wonder if the lack of knowledge leads to a subdiagnosis, because few notifications are made.

In conclusion, it is necessary to have both national and international public policies aimed at improving the knowledge of epidemiology, symptomatology (human and animal) and the notification process; in order to obtain consistent and representative information on the prevalence of these diseases.

I hope you read it and enjoy it as much as I did!
