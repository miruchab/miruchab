[ENG] Analysis of Chagas disease

November 25, 2019

At the end of my degree in Veterinary Sciences at the University of Buenos Aires, I had to submit a final paper on a subject related to my Supervised Professional Practices. I chose the area of “Preventive Medicine and Public Health”.

It struck me that it would be a good idea to show a few of R’s qualities as applied to Veterinary Sciences, particularly in the field of Veterinary Epidemiology. This is an extension of the analysis carried out for this paper, which was somewhat shorter since it had to be submitted to the School authorities in the format of a scientific poster, accompanied by a report and a PowerPoint presentation. As the point was to show the application of R, I also chose a simple dataset so as not to fill the paper with too much information.

For this purpose, I searched for an official dataset by the Modernization Department of the Argentine government. I found one on reports of acute vectorial Chagas disease in the entire Argentine Republic, between 1996 and 2017.

Chagas disease (or Chagas-Mazza disease) is a neglected and potentially fatal tropical parasitic disease caused by the flagellate protozoan Trypanosoma cruzi. This disease is a zoonosis affecting several different vertebrates. Dogs are important carriers/reservoirs of the disease, not because they transmit the infection directly to humans, but because, as they live in close proximity, when they are bitten by the bug or vinchuca (mainly Triatoma infestans), the same insect can bite humans.

Data tidying

chagas <- chagas %>%
        rename_all(~str_replace_all(.,"casos_chagas_",""))

chagas <- chagas %>% 
        mutate(indice_tiempo = as.Date(indice_tiempo, format = "%Y"))

chagas <- separate(data = chagas, col = "indice_tiempo", into = c("year", NULL, NULL))

chagas <- pivot_longer(data = chagas, 
                       cols = ciudad_autonoma_buenos_aires:tierra_del_fuego, 
                       values_to = "cases")

chagas_province <- chagas %>%
        filter(cases > 0) %>%
        select(-argentina) %>%
        group_by(year, name) %>%
        summarise(total_cases = sum(cases))

Some basic modifications were made, such as removing months and days from the dates (in all cases they appeared as, for example, “01-01-2000” instead of “year 2000”, which is more useful). I also simplified the names of the columns that refer to the province name, and reduced all the variables (one per province) to one “cases” variable.

I also created another dataframe called “chagas_province”, that I’ll use later on.

The first step would be to see how the prevalence of this disease progressed throughout the Argentine territory.

You can see the downward trend in reported cases over time, which is great!

The next interesting thing would be to know in which provinces these cases occurred, since this disease is endemic in the north of the country. This happens because the vinchuca is found in 70% of the national territory (from the north of the country to the south of Rio Negro) and in larger numbers in hot and dry climate areas.

As can be seen, most cases reported historically (or at least since 1996) were concentrated in the Province of Santiago del Estero (which makes sense since it is in the endemic area). However, it is interesting to note the progressive decrease in the number of reports, both in this province and in Argentina in general.

Something that could be useful, although the chart won’t look as pretty, is a more objective comparison among provinces. Thus, using the same scale for all of them, it will be possible to show the number of cases by province and the distribution in each one over time.

What’s lost in aesthetics is won in information. Santiago del Estero comes first in terms of number of cases, but there was a dramatic decrease in prevalence around 2005 (guesstimated). I find the case of Santiago del Estero fascinating, as the fall in prevalence is remarkable. I’d like to see a chart only for this province, because I want to know in what year this downswing took place and I want to be able to find out what public health measures were taken to achieve it.

Chart for Santiago del Estero

        theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45))

It seems that my guesstimate wasn’t entirely accurate, because the sudden decrease in prevalence took place starting in 2009 rather than 2005 (this is why it’s important to use reliable sources). I started googling what happened in Santiago del Estero in 2008 (and a little earlier as well).

I kept on investigantig and found that the PAHO (Pan American Health Organization) designated the Santiago del Estero Chagas Center as a WHO (World Health Organization) Collaborating Centre in 2015, for its work in the eradication of this disease in 6 departments of this province.

Finally, on the website of this province’s Ministry of Health, I found out about an award given to one of the founders of the Santiago del Estero Chagas Center. He explains that in 2005 it was decided to reformulate the Chagas program, due to the fact that all 27 departments of the province reported the presence of this disease. This could be the reason why only 4 years later there was a decrease in prevalence, and only 10 years later the Santiago del Estero Chagas Center was declared a WHO Collaborating Centre.

In summary: Chagas disease management is very important in Argentina as the north of the country is an endemic area. This management requires interdisciplinary work, in which we, veterinarians, must be involved. As we have seen in the data shown, and given the fact that it is a disease with a high degree of prevalence that has as a reservoir one of the most common domestic animals, it is necessary to highlight the importance of public policies and the actions of entities of veterinary health that are dedicated both to spread information and to combat this disease’s dissemination.
